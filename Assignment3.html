Part III: Project Description

A. Overview

Topic

The proposed project aims to develop an automatic data collecting system for aquaculture ponds that will provide valuable information on water quality and pond conditions. This system will utilize IoT technology to monitor various parameters such as temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen, turbidity, ammonia, and nitrate levels in real-time (Udanor et al. 2022). By analyzing the collected data, the system will generate necessary feedback for farmers, including real-time pond situations, future pond condition forecasts, and guidance on maintaining the pond. This scientific approach to pond maintenance will ultimately enhance the efficiency of the aquaculture industry.

The project will involve integrating multiple sensors into the aquaculture pond system to continuously monitor and record water quality parameters (Vo et al. 2021). These sensors will be connected to a data acquisition and processing system, which will be responsible for collecting, analyzing, and providing actionable insights to the farmers. Motivation

The current absence of standardized pond monitoring protocols within the aquaculture industry fosters an environment of perilous ambiguity for farmers. Operating in a data vacuum, their decision-making regarding the aquatic health of their stock remains susceptible to misinterpretations and reactive interventions. This lack of proactive understanding directly contributes to the unfortunate reality of mass crustacean and fish mortalities, often stemming from undetected fluctuations in crucial water quality parameters. Consequently, these blind spots within the production chain perpetuate the existence of subpar aquaculture feed, optimized for hypothetical ideal conditions rather than tailored to the specific needs of diverse pond ecosystems. This detrimental cycle, driven by data paucity and inadequate feed formulations, not only jeopardizes the welfare of farmed aquatic animals but also poses a potential threat to consumer health. Hence, the development and implementation of standardized pond monitoring systems becomes an imperative not just for economic stability but also for ensuring the ethical and sustainable future of aquaculture.

Landscape

Currently, the Vietnamese aquaculture industry does not have readily accessible automated mechanisms for collecting information, as reported by reliable sources. There is currently no product available for purchase that is similar to this. While farmers have options like manual test kits or lab analysis, both suffer from drawbacks. Kits are labor-intensive and provide limited data, while labs offer slow turnaround and potentially inaccurate results. This leaves farmers struggling to fully grasp pond dynamics and make informed decisions. Existing tools exist for measuring individual pond parameters, but they operate in silos, failing to offer a holistic view or actionable insights. This is where our project offers a revolutionary leap forward. Our product automatically collects critical water values, analyzes them scientifically, and translates them into precise forecasts and practical advice for farmers. This real-time, integrated approach differentiates us from any existing options by empowering farmers with timely, data-driven insights for optimized pond management, a stark contrast to the fragmented and delayed information currently available.

C. Plans and Progress

Our project began when one of our teammates was researching the aquaculture situation in South Vietnam. He found out that farmers in South Vietnam still use primitive techniques to maintain their fish pond which is very ineffective and unreliable. From that observation, our teammate proposed to us his idea about an automatic aquaculture monitoring system. After careful thinking and evaluation, we pushed forward with the idea of a system that can automatically collect data from the fish’s ponds, analyze it and send feedback to the farmers using scientific methods. How the system works is that it will collect 2 most important variables in the fish’s pond: the pH level and the temperature level. After that, we will add additional theoretical conclusions and combine with on-site observation to determine the situation of the pond. This data collecting process will take roughly 8 to 10 weeks.

The original idea for the project is that we will have 3 types of sensors to measure the fish’s pond condition, that is: pH, temperature and oxygen level. But after we did a lot of research, we came to a conclusion that have the oxygen level sensor included in the system is unfeasible because the production cost and maintenance cost is too high. Thus, our system will only have 2 types of sensors for the time being. Up until now, we are currently working on the software of the system. We decided that using Java would be the best coding language for the system because most of our teammates have experience with it. And for the microcontroller platform we decided to use Arduino due to its ease of use and compatibility with various sensors.

For the hardware setup, we all agreed to use the Aqualine 10K Temperature Sensor from the Aqualine brand, assembled with an Arduino Uno and a pH sensor that has analog output. The sensor will be connected to the appropriate pins on the Arduino, and the system is powered with a stable power supply. A basic Arduino code was developed to read pH and temperature values from the sensors. The code included functions for reading analog signals from the pH sensor and digital temperature readings from the DS18B20 sensor. The Arduino was connected to a computer via USB, and the collected data was displayed on the Serial Monitor. This allowed for real-time monitoring of pH and temperature values.

However, we have been facing a lot of challenges and dead-ends. One of our most difficult challenges right now is connecting with the farmers. Because farmers in South Vietnam are usually very conservative, they do not want strange things to be put in their fish’s pond so we had a hard time convincing farmers to use our system in the data collecting period. Right now, we have the lab testing environment but because the on-site environment is so much different so without a proper test field, it is hard to acquire meaningful data for our system.

Another big set back is that we do not have enough data to predict the longevity of our system. Naturally, when sensors are put into a hazardous environment, it will deteriorate and lose its accuracy. In order to prevent this, we can choose to use a more expensive and tougher sensor for the job but we soon realized it was impossible due to the total system production price being too high. Another way is that we figure out the deterioration rate of the sensor and react appropriately, either through adjusting the code to suit the deterioration rate of the system. This solution is the most sensible but because our system does not have enough on-site experience we can not give a precise result about the deterioration rate. Under normal lab conditions, our sensor will start to deteriorate in about week 6 of constant exposure to the environment.

We also discuss another solution is that based on the approximate sensor longevity in the lab condition, we will have members do the manual maintenance. This solution will be the most effective at keeping our system functional because we can check and clean every hazardous chemical the sensors have been exposed to. But this solution also comes with its own tremendous drawback that is manpower. In the lab environment, we should have enough members to do the maintenance consistently but when we try on-site testing, we are afraid we are shorted- staff to do maintenance for every machine in every pond. At the time being, we are still using this method because of its flexibility but in the future if we want to publish our system, we will have to think carefully about a more long-term solution.

I.Risks

Upon assessing our project and its potential risks, several issues have emerged notably. Firstly, the underlying complex nature of the pond creates challenges for the sensor system to accurately diagnose and categorize abnormalities in farmers’ ponds. This makes the sensor system vulnerable to providing inaccurate or only partially accurate information, due to the intricacies involved. To mitigate this, our team can extend the data collection period to increase the variety of scenarios the system encounters, effectively training the system to be smarter to deal with anomalous situations.

The stability and safety of the sensor is also susceptible to the often toxic environment of the pond. The hazardous nature of the pond leads to decreased lifespan for the sensors, leading to potential system instability. This risk will be inevitable, however solutions to this are simple. In the initial phases, we will conduct highly frequent manual maintenance, record average longevity of the sensors and improve these deficiencies early on.

Moreover, the typical conservative mindset of many Vietnamese farmers also poses an additional layer of risk. For a long time, farmers are often skeptical if not resistant to new technologies that disrupt their long-standing practices of manually monitoring their fish ponds. This skepticism was further exacerbated during the COVID-19 pandemic, resulting in massive drops of aquaculture sales, making farmers less likely to invest in technologies that help streamline and enhance their quality of work. To tackle this concern, we may explore the option of improving our system’s User Interface, making it more easy and intuitive for farmers to use, making them less resistant to these sophisticated tgiut

J.Group processes and communications

  • Our group will mostly be communicating via online means. This is to optimize the time spent for building the actual product itself, coding & testing, instead of wasting time on commutes for frequent face-to-face meetings. However, there will be exceptions, where there will be meetings about hardware related discussions, where actual demo testing of the hardware is required.

This is because we will be designing codes, building the hardware and integrating the software with hardware, and this process necessitates constant feedback and adjustments, and clear and consistent communication to ensure a smooth development workflow. These meetings could range from online joint coding sessions or code review, architecture and design discussions or problem-solving brainstorming.
  • However, at later stages, notably during Week 5 to Week 14, our group’s meetings will be much more loosened, due to this phase mostly being about data collection and constant analysis, which could be done without the need for excessive meetings. We expect that these meetings will occur at minimum 1-2 online meetings per week to discuss our data findings together, and make adjustments to our project where necessary.
  • In terms of communication channels, our originally thought of paid platforms to create a professional environment, but due to budget constraints, we are considering free alternatives. For team meetings, our team prefers to use Microsoft Teams which is provided exclusively for free for RMIT students, with full features. As for formal project communications, our team will use a platform called “Slack”, which is a cloud-based collaboration platform to streamline communication and enhance teamwork within organizations. We found that Slack was intuitive to use, commonly used by IT professionals in the workplace, and offers basic functions like messaging, file sharing, project planning, and tools integration, for free. For informal conversations between team members (like settling on a meetup place, sharing useful information to each other, etc.), we will use Facebook’s Messenger.

What happens if a team member does not respond to communications?

  • One of the most striking causes of ineffective communications (and hence, member unresponsiveness) in teams is having unclear objectives & roles.

  • To mitigate this, in our team’s initial meeting, we will set clear project objectives, define clear scope and limits, map out a coherent execution timeline with clear phases and measurable objectives, which are then delegated fairly among members of the team.
  • We will also together set clear working expectations, ensuring everyone understands the required work ethic, and the expected number of meetings that our team should have.
  • These measures will help ensure an effective communication environment where our team members are enabled to contribute, deterring unresponsiveness.

However, if we implemented these prevention tactics, but a member still does not respond, we will:

  • Try communicating with the team member via another channel like text messaging, phone call, sID email, or attempt contacting that member’s acquaintances for assistance.
  • In the worst case, we will seek support from either RMIT lecturers or contacting the school to help us get in contact with that member.
  • In the worst case, the member in question chose to intentionally ignore communications. If this happens, we will suspend this person from the team, and our team will have a follow-up meeting to rearrange the scope of work and responsibilities of the remaining members.

1.The Finance Specialist (FS)

The FS role is the nearest scope of work to what is commonly the “CFO” or “Chief Financial Officer”. This role in general will help our project with financial planning, reporting and analysis, and ensuring the appropriate use of resources in the project. In startups, CFOs often play a crucial role in fundraising, helping to instill investor confidence, by helping to gather critical information about the organization, and providing income and expenditure projections. CFOs in typical startups also work tirelessly to provide the leadership team with vital information, to help the rest of the team make informed, cautious and strategic decisions. This applies also to our project, where the finance specialist will also play a pivotal role in the scaling of our project, by assuming similar responsibilities.

The FS in our team has to be someone careful and conscientious. The person also has to be highly analytical, and has to possess a strategic and calculating mindset. These traits will help the FS think logically about the project’s resources and expenses, help him/her work effectively with large quantities of data and complex information, and help this person think about both the short-term and long-term resource needs of the project. An FS who has all of these traits, will undoubtedly help our project scale smoothly.

In terms of technical expertise, we researched that a finance specialist with an “ACCA” certificate (Association of Chartered Certified Accountants) is sufficient for our project. Obtaining the ACCA requires a deep comprehension of theoretical finance concepts and proficiency in finance-related skills such as financial modeling, data analysis, valuation, accounting, and risk management – which is highly required for our project and for dealing with venture capitalists. Other certifications exist such as the CFA or CPA, but in our group’s opinion, these certifications demand very high compensation, which is not feasible for our project.

2.Business Development Specialist (BDS)

The Business Development Specialist will have the highest set of responsibilities in the entire project, similar to how a “CEO” or “Chief Executive Officer” has the highest leadership authority in a large organization. This specialist needs to be capable in various aspects of business including management, finance, and market strategy.

On a general level, the BDS will have the highest responsibility for handling internal and external affairs of this project in the scaling phase. When rapidly expanding an IT project, four stakeholders emerge as the most important: the internal team, customers, partners and investors.

Internally, the BDS will oversee the project’s overall management, ensuring the team effectively executes on the tasks that aligns with the team’s objectives in a disciplined and timely manner. This person is also responsible for aligning incentives between other personnel to properly motivate and push the entire project team.

Externally, this role is in charge of conducting market research, to gauge the market’s (i.e. aquaculture farmers) demand levels, or to find new markets that the project can penetrate into. The BDS is held accountable for selling the project idea to customers too. This role is also responsible for forming strategic partnerships that accelerates the project’s growth, and help connect the project with potential investors and venture capitalists to fundraise for the project’s initial and growth stages.

In short, the BDS needs to possess a rich understanding of business, to efficiently and effectively manage the internal team well, and profitably engage external connections.

In terms of characteristics, our team needs a BDS who is strategic, visionary, charismatic, and has a deep understanding of market dynamics. Being strategic is crucial to any top leadership position, as people often follow these insights from the top leader. This type of leader has to have charisma, as it often attracts and compels people to work for the project, or for the leader themselves. He/she also needs to comprehend the market well, grasping core pain points, and intuit when an opportunity arises in the marketplace to capitalize on. All of these traits make for a powerful leader, capable of expanding the project to the next level.

In terms of technical expertise required, our team figured that someone with a degree like “Bachelor of Business Management” with a few years of prior work-leadership experience is sufficient for our project’s needs. Someone with this certificate and this level of experience should have a reasonable understanding of business strategies, market dynamics, management frameworks, financial statements,... to help scale our project. We had considered those with MBAs or Masters of Business Administration as the much better alternative for hiring, but those with this degree demand very high compensation, which would be unaffordable and unnecessary for our project.

3. Aquaculture Biologist (AB)

During and after the process of creating the system, the Aquaculture Biologist plays a crucial role in inputting and updating the system with accurate scientific theories and giving examples about the pond’s values data analysis to the system so that the system can start producing appropriate analysis and prediction. The AB is also responsible for acknowledging any abnormalities about the pond’s condition and providing appropriate predictions to the system to combat it when it occurs.

In terms of characteristics, since our AB would need to be on-site a lot of the time for observing and comparing if the pond’s condition is aligned with the system prediction and working with the IT specialist to transform the scientific information into the system data, our team needs an AB who is quite out-going, cooperative and have physical . The AB should also have good Analytical Skills, Problem-Solving Abilities and Environmental Awareness. Having good Analytical Skills will increase the strength in interpreting data related to water quality, fish health, and environmental conditions. Problem-Solving Abilities to identify issues within aquaculture systems and develop effective solutions and Environmental Awareness for understanding of environmental impacts and a commitment to sustainable and responsible aquaculture practices.

In terms of technical expertise required, our team reckons someone that has knowledge in Aquaculture Biology, Water Quality Management, Pathology and Health Management and Aquatic Ecology. Aquaculture Biology for in-depth knowledge of fish biology, physiology, and behavior to optimize conditions for growth and health. Water Quality Management to have the expertise in monitoring and managing water quality parameters, including pH, temperature and dissolved oxygen levels. Pathology and Health Management for understanding of fish diseases, parasitology, and health management practices within aquaculture. And Aquatic Ecology for the knowledge of aquatic ecosystems and their interactions to promote a balanced and sustainable environment.

4.IT Specialist (ITS)

Just like the Aquaculture Biologist, the IT Specialist also plays an important role during and after the process of creating the system. The ITS is responsible for creating the sensor hardware for the fish’ pond, creating and code for the sensor hardware, creating the code for the main system and creating a communication channel between the main system and the sensor hardware. ITS is also responsible for transforming the scientific knowledge from the AB into the main system hardware and main system maintaining and updating.

In terms of characteristics, our ITS should have Problem-Solving Skills, Adaptability, Team Collaboration and Client-Focused Approach. Problem-Solving Skills for strong analytical and problem-solving abilities to address technical challenges and optimize system performance. Adaptability to adapt to evolving technologies and the specific needs of the aquaculture industry. Team Collaboration to collaborate with interdisciplinary teams, including AB, BDS and FS. And Client-Focused Approach to have a customer-focus mindset to understand and address the needs of end-users, aquaculture managers, and stakeholders.

In terms of technical expertise required, our team reckons someone that has knowledge in Programming Languages, Database Management, Web Development, Data Analytics and Mobile Application Development. Programming Languages for the proficiency in programming languages such as Python, Java, or C++ for developing and maintaining software components of the monitoring system. Database Management for the expertise in database design and management (e.g., MySQL, MongoDB) to store and retrieve sensor data efficiently. Data Analytics for the proficiency in data analytics tools to analyze historical data trends, identify patterns, and provide actionable insights for aquaculture management.
